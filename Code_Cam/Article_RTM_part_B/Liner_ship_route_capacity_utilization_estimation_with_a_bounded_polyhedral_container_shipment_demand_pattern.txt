"Transportation Research Part B: MethodologicalLiner ship route capacity utilization estimation with a bounded polyhedral container shipment demand patternAbstract This paper aims to estimate capacity utilization of a liner ship route with a bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern, arising in the liner container shipping industry. The proposed maximum and minimum liner ship route capacity utilization problems are formulated as a linear programming model and a min\u2013max model, respectively. We examine two fundamental properties of the min\u2013max model. These two nice properties enable us to develop two \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithms for solving the min\u2013max model, which find a globally \u03b5-optimal solution by iteratively cutting off the bounded polyhedral container shipment demand set with a cut. The latter algorithm overcomes non-convexity of the remaining feasible demand set generated by the former algorithm via a novel hyperplane cut. Each hyperplane cut can assure that the current vertex of the polyhedral demand set is cut off, whereas solutions that may improve the current one by more than a factor of \u03b5 are retained. Highlights \u25ba Formulate a bounded polyhedral set for uncertain container shipment demand. \u25ba Calculate the maximum and minimum capacity utilization of a liner ship route. \u25ba Propose a global \u03b5-optimal algorithm that maintains convexity of the demand set. Abstract This paper aims to estimate capacity utilization of a liner ship route with a bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern, arising in the liner container shipping industry. The proposed maximum and minimum liner ship route capacity utilization problems are formulated as a linear programming model and a min\u2013max model, respectively. We examine two fundamental properties of the min\u2013max model. These two nice properties enable us to develop two \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithms for solving the min\u2013max model, which find a globally \u03b5-optimal solution by iteratively cutting off the bounded polyhedral container shipment demand set with a cut. The latter algorithm overcomes non-convexity of the remaining feasible demand set generated by the former algorithm via a novel hyperplane cut. Each hyperplane cut can assure that the current vertex of the polyhedral demand set is cut off, whereas solutions that may improve the current one by more than a factor of \u03b5 are retained. Extensive numerical experiments for problems larger than those encountered in real applications demonstrate the computational efficacy of the latter algorithm. Keywords Liner shipping Capacity utilization Polyhedral container shipment demand \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm 1 Introduction A liner ship route (or shipping service) refers to a rotation of ports called by a string of containerships with a given frequency, such as once a week. A vital planning decision for a liner shipping company is to deploy containerships with a high percentage of capacity utilization on an existing ship route or on a new shipping service with respect to a predicted or estimated container shipment demand pattern. Maersk Line, for example, signed a contract in February 2011 for ten of the world\u2019s largest containerships, with an option to purchase another 20 (JOC, 2012). Each of these mega-containerships has a capacity of 18,000 TEUs (20-foot equivalent units), 16% larger (2500 TEU) than the Emma Maersk, the largest containership currently in operation. Mega-containerships may bring in greater profit for a liner container shipping company and/or result in a lower freight rate for shippers because of their economies of scale in ship size and energy efficiency (McLellan, 1997; Cullinane and Khanna, 2000). However, deploying a string of mega-containerships on a liner ship route makes profit only if the capacity of these mega-containerships is highly utilized, namely, there is sufficient container shipment demand to cater to these mega-containerships. Maersk Line has explained that it is buying the mega-containerships to position itself to profit from an anticipated 5\u20138% growth in the Asia-Europe trade through to 2015. A liner shipping company often targets emerging container shipment markets by launching new shipping services to gain more profit and maintain its competitiveness. For example, a liner shipping company is concerned with the following questions: is it economically viable to add a new ship route connecting East Africa to a hub port in the Middle East (e.g., Salalah) to attract African container shipment demand for the liner shipping company? What if a new shipping service from Australia to Europe is launched, so that containers from Australia to Europe do not need to be transshipped at the port of Singapore? Before launching a new ship route, in reality, the liner shipping company has to assess its capacity utilization. The new liner ship route is not commercially viable if its capacity utilization is too low. It is, therefore, of practical importance for a liner shipping company to conduct a feasibility study on capacity utilization before deploying containerships on an existing ship route or launching a new ship route based on a future container shipment demand estimated or predicted between two ports. However, a good estimation of future port-to-port container shipment demand is in general not available; a good estimation here means that the estimated or predicted container ship demand matches accurately the real future demand in the case of deterministic demand or the probability density function in the case of stochastic demand. By contrast, the liner shipping company frequently obtains lower and upper bounds for the future port-to-port container shipment demand or sum of container shipment demands from and to some ports of the ship route. For example, if container shipment volume from Asia to Europe increases by 5\u20138% annually through to 2015, the lower and upper bounds of the total container shipment demand from Asian ports to European ports can be derived based on the current demand. These correlated container shipment demands between ports of the ship route, in general, form a bounded polyhedral set, referred to as the bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern in this study. With a bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern, the feasibility study can estimate the maximum and minimum capacity utilization of the ship route instead of directly assessing its profitability, in view of the challenge of precisely estimating the future freight rate. In practice, capacity utilization is an important indicator of the quality of liner shipping services as it is one of the main determinants of a liner shipping company\u2019s profitability (Agarwal and Ergun, 2008). It can thus be concluded that the liner ship route capacity utilization estimation for a bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern could be an important research issue in the liner shipping industry. This paper aims to develop optimization models and design efficient solution algorithms for this problem. Besides the practical importance of the proposed liner ship route capacity utilization estimation with a bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern, its academic novelty can be convincingly confirmed after a short and comprehensive literature review below. The decision problems associated with liner shipping services can be classified into strategic, tactical and operational levels as per their planning horizons (Ronen, 1983, 1993; Christiansen et al., 2004, 2007). Strategic level decisions include the ship fleet size and mix, the market area to service, and alliance strategies. The decision problems at the tactical level incorporate liner shipping network design, fleet deployment, sailing speed determination, and schedule construction. In the network design problems, decisions regarding the deployment of ships and their speed must also be made. The sailing speed is usually assumed to be fixed in order to simplify the problem. The fleet deployment problem determines the optimal ships to assign on each ship route by assuming that their port rotations are given. The sailing speed optimization problem also requires fixed port rotations and aims to obtain the optimal trade-off between ship cost and bunker cost. The schedule construction problem determines the arrival and departure date at each port of call. The operational-level decisions include cargo selection and routing, sailing speed adjustment, and ad hoc services. Mathematical programming approaches have been successfully used to formulate these optimal decision problems. Table 1 summarizes the decision problems addressed by the optimization methods in the literature and the container shipment demand pattern assumed in each of the studies. The above-mentioned literature review indicates that the proposed liner ship capacity estimation problem with a bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern is a new academic research issue with important practical relevance. We examine the maximum and minimum capacity utilization of a liner ship route based on the polyhedral container shipment demand pattern. The maximum and minimum capacity utilization corresponds to the best and worst outcomes, respectively. As a result, they provide useful information for the decisions on fleet deployment and ship route design for liner shipping companies. Therefore, the liner ship capacity estimation problem lies mainly at the tactical planning level (fleet deployment, ship route design) but may also arise at the strategic planning level (order new mega-containerships or not). The minimum capacity utilization problem is computationally challenging because it is non-convex and is proved to be NP-hard. We examine the fundamental properties of the minimum capacity utilization problem by taking advantage of its special structure resulting from practical considerations that arise from the liner container shipping industry. Based on these nice properties, we design an \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm and its improvement. The improved \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm is able to obtain a globally \u03b5-optimal solution by iteratively cutting off part of the container shipment demand polytope with hyperplane cuts. Each hyperplane cut is generated in a novel manner such that the current vertex of the demand polytope is cut off, while solutions that may improve the current one by more than a factor of \u03b5 are retained. Extensive numerical experiments for problems larger than those encountered in real applications demonstrate the computational efficacy of the improved \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 defines capacity utilization of a liner ship route and introduces the bounded polyhedral container shipment demand pattern. Section 3 formulates maximum and minimum capacity utilization models and analyzes the fundamental properties of the minimum capacity utilization model. Based on the properties developed in Section 3, Section 4 proposes an \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm and its improvement for solving the minimum capacity utilization model. Section 5 carries out numerical experiments based on an Asia\u2013Europe liner ship route and randomly generated data to assess the computational performance of the proposed improved \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm. We therefore conclude that the proposed improved \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm can be applied to solve real-life problems efficiently and effectively. 6 Conclusions We have proposed a novel and practical research issue arising in the liner container shipping industry to estimate the liner ship route capacity utilization. To take into account the uncertainty in container shipment demand, we formulate the container shipment demand as a bounded polyhedral set and investigate the maximum and minimum capacity utilization of a liner ship route. The maximum and minimum capacity utilization provides useful information for decisions on fleet deployment and ship route design for liner shipping companies. The minimum capacity utilization problem is computationally challenging because it is non-convex and is NP-hard. We examine the fundamental properties of the minimum capacity utilization problem by taking advantage of its special structure resulting from practical considerations that arise from the liner container shipping industry. Based on these nice properties, we design an \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm and its improvement. The improved \u03b5-optimal global optimization algorithm is able to obtain a globally \u03b5-optimal solution by iteratively cutting off part of the container shipment demand polytope with hyperplane cuts. Each hyperplane cut is generated such that the current vertex of the demand polytope is cut off, while solutions that may improve the current one by more than a factor of \u03b5 are retained. The novelty and success of the algorithm lie in that the feasible region of container shipment demand remains convex throughout the algorithm. Extensive numerical experiments for problems larger than those encountered in real applications demonstrate the computational efficacy of the algorithm. Finally, it should be pointed out that the methodology proposed in this paper may be extended to investigate the capacity utilization of a liner shipping network and that of a public transit service network (e.g., Carey, 1999; Eberlein et al., 1998; Daganzo and Pilachowski, 2011; Xuan et al., 2011).
